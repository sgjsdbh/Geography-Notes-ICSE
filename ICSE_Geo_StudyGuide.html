Climate of India
Monsoon, seasons, temperature and rainfall patterns — one of the most frequently asked topics in the paper.
04
🗂 Overview
🌏
Climate Type
India has a Tropical Monsoon Climate. Characterized by seasonal reversal of winds, hot summers, and a distinct rainy season.
🌡️
Temperature Range
Varies from below 0°C in Himalayan regions to above 48°C in Thar Desert. Coastal areas have moderate, equable climate.
🌧️
Rainfall Range
From 11 cm/year (Jaisalmer, Rajasthan) to 1,187 cm/year (Mawsynram, Meghalaya — highest in world).
💨
Monsoon Wind
A periodic wind that completely reverses its direction seasonally. SW in summer (rain-bearing), NE in winter (dry).

Favourite "give a reason" question: Why does Mangaluru get more rain than Chennai in summer? Mangaluru is on the windward side of Western Ghats; Chennai is on the leeward (rain shadow) side.
🗓 The Four Seasons of India
☀️
Summer
March – May
• Temp rises to 40–48°C in NW India
• Low pressure develops over NW
• Hot dry winds called Loo blow in N India
• Dust storms common in Rajasthan
• Nor'westers (Kalbaisakhi) in WB & NE
🌧️
Monsoon
June – September
• SW Monsoon arrives (Kerala ~June 1)
• 2 branches: Arabian Sea + Bay of Bengal
• 75–90% of India's annual rainfall
• Humid, heavy rain, floods possible
• "Burst of monsoon" on arrival
🍂
Retreating Monsoon
October – November
• SW winds retreat southward
• NE winds pick up moisture over Bay of Bengal
• Tamil Nadu & SE coast get heavy rain
• Cyclones form in Bay of Bengal
• Temp begins to fall
❄️
Winter
December – February
• NW winds blow (cold, dry)
• Temperature drops; frost in N plains
• Western Disturbances bring rain to NW
• Dense fog in N India
• Pleasant in peninsular India
💨 Monsoon Mechanism ⭐ Always Asked
The mechanism of monsoon is one of the highest-frequency questions in ICSE Geography. Understand the steps, don't just memorise them.
🌞 Why does the SW Monsoon occur? (Summer → Monsoon) ▼
1
Sun shifts North

After March, the sun moves northwards. The ITCZ (Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone) also shifts north.

2
Low pressure over NW India

The Thar Desert and NW Indian plains heat up rapidly, creating an intense low pressure area (depression).

3
Winds rush in from ocean

High pressure exists over the southern Indian Ocean. Winds blow from high → low pressure, i.e., from the ocean towards India.

4
Coriolis Effect

Earth's rotation deflects these winds to the right in the N hemisphere. Southern hemisphere SE trade winds cross equator and become SW winds.

5
Two Branches form

Arabian Sea Branch → hits Western Ghats → heavy rain on west coast → crosses Deccan, reaches N India. Bay of Bengal Branch → enters through NE India → Assam, Meghalaya (Mawsynram) → moves W & N.

6
Why Mawsynram gets most rain

Situated in funnel-shaped Khasi Hills, Meghalaya. The Bay of Bengal branch is channelled and forced to rise sharply → extremely heavy orographic rainfall (~1187 cm/year).

🍂 Retreating Monsoon — Why does Tamil Nadu get rain in Oct–Nov? ▼
  • By October, the low pressure over NW India weakens as the sun moves south.
  • The SW Monsoon starts to retreat from NW India towards SE India.
  • NE trade winds blow over the Bay of Bengal and pick up moisture.
  • These moisture-laden NE winds strike Tamil Nadu and SE coast → heavy rain.
  • Chennai receives most of its rain during this season, not during SW Monsoon.
This is why Goa gets more rain than Puducherry in summer — Goa is on the windward side of Western Ghats; Puducherry is on the leeward side and misses the SW Monsoon.

Soil Resources
Types, distribution, characteristics, and how to protect our most precious layer of land.
05
🗂 Four Main Soil Types
You must know: colour, texture, key minerals, crops, distribution state — for all 4 soils. These are tested every year.
🟡 Alluvial Soil — Most widespread, most fertile ▼

Formation: Deposited by rivers (Ganga, Brahmaputra, Indus and their tributaries) over thousands of years.

Distribution: Indo-Gangetic Plains, river deltas (Krishna, Godavari, Kaveri), coastal plains — about 40% of India's land.

Two types:

  • Khadar — New alluvial, near rivers, lighter in colour, more fertile, renewed every year by floods
  • Bhangar — Old alluvial, away from rivers, darker, contains calcium carbonate (kankar) nodules

Texture

Deficient in

Rice, wheat, sugarcane, cotton, jute, oilseeds
Alluvial soil = India's most important soil. Supports agriculture for over 600 million people.
⚫ Black Soil (Regur) — Cotton's best friend ▼

Formation: Weathering of basic igneous rocks (basalt/Deccan trap rocks). That's why it's also called Regur or cotton soil.

Distribution: Deccan Plateau — Maharashtra, Gujarat, MP, parts of Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana.

Special Features:

  • Self-ploughing — Cracks when dry, mixes its own lower layers
  • High moisture retention — Holds water for long after rains
  • Swells when wet, shrinks when dry
Colour
Dark black to grey (iron titaniferous magnetite)
Texture

Rich in

Deficient in
Nitrogen, phosphorus, organic matter
Major crops
Cotton (ideal!), sugarcane, jowar, groundnut, sunflower
Black soil is ideal for cotton because it retains moisture well and cotton needs long periods of dry weather at harvest time — black soil provides both.
🔴 Red Soil — Iron-rich, needs fertilizers ▼

Formation: Weathering of ancient crystalline and metamorphic rocks (gneiss, granite).

Distribution: Tamil Nadu, Goa, Karnataka, SE Maharashtra, Andhra Pradesh, Odisha, Jharkhand, MP — mostly in Deccan and Eastern peninsula.

Note: Appears red due to diffusion of iron oxide. When hydrated (in subsoil), it appears yellow.

Major crops

Dry farming (without much water) is preferred in red soil areas because red soil is porous, drains quickly, and doesn't retain water well.
🟤 Laterite Soil — Leached, hard, useful as building material ▼

Formation: Formed in high temperature, high rainfall conditions by leaching — heavy rain washes away minerals like silica, leaving behind iron and aluminium oxides.

Distribution: Western Ghats, parts of Eastern Ghats, NE India, Odisha, Maharashtra highlands, Karnataka.

Special Feature: Soft and porous when wet; extremely hard when dry — hence used as building material (bricks).

Colour

Texture

Rich in

Deficient in

Major crops
Tea, coffee, cashew, coconut (with manure)
Physical characteristic of laterite soil that's often asked: It's hard and durable when dry → used as building material (bricks). Also: it is the result of leaching process.
⚠️ Soil Erosion
🌳
Human Causes
Deforestation, overgrazing, faulty farming, shifting cultivation (jhum), construction activities
🌊
Natural Causes
Running water (most common!), wind erosion (arid regions), glaciers, waves, mass movements
🔬
Types of Erosion
Sheet erosion: thin top layer washed away uniformly. Rill/Gully: deep cuts formed. Wind erosion: in deserts.

Natural Vegetation
India's forests — types, distribution, importance and conservation.
06
🌳 Why Forests Matter
💧
Water Cycle
Trees absorb rainwater; roots prevent surface runoff; forests maintain groundwater level
🌱
Soil Protection
Root systems bind soil; tree canopy breaks raindrop impact; prevents erosion
🌡️
Climate Regulation
Absorb CO₂; release O₂; regulate temperature; increase humidity and rainfall
🐯
Biodiversity
Habitat for wildlife, birds, insects. India is a mega-biodiversity country with 8% of world's species
🪵
Economic Value
Timber, fuel wood, gum, resin, rubber, medicine, honey, lac — major forest products
🏘️
Livelihood
Over 100 million tribal people depend directly on forests for food, shelter and income
🗺 Types of Vegetation
All 5 forest types are regularly tested — know rainfall, location, trees, and one special feature for each.
🌴 Tropical Evergreen Forests — Dense, year-round green ▼

Rainfall needed: More than 200 cm/year, no distinct dry season

Temperature: High (25–30°C) and uniform year-round

Distribution: Western Ghats (coastal strip), Andaman & Nicobar Islands, NE India (Assam, Meghalaya)

Key Features:

  • Trees do NOT shed all leaves at the same time (no distinct leafless period)
  • Multi-layered dense canopy — sunlight barely reaches ground
  • Very diverse species — economically valuable hardwood trees
  • Trees: Ebony, Mahogany, Rosewood, Rubber, Cinchona
Why are they difficult to exploit commercially?

1. Extremely dense — difficult to access
2. No roads/transport
3. Huge variety of species — few trees of each kind per acre
4. Very hard, heavy timber — difficult to float on rivers
🍃 Tropical Deciduous Forests — Most widespread in India ▼

Rainfall: 70–200 cm/year. Distribution: ~60% of India's forested area — most widespread type. Found across Peninsular India, foothills of Himalayas, NE India.

🌧️ Moist Deciduous (100–200 cm)

  • Teak, Sal, Bamboo, Sandalwood, Shisham
  • Eastern slopes of Western Ghats, NE India, N Odisha, Jharkhand
  • Commercially most important — teak is most valuable

☀️ Dry Deciduous (70–100 cm)

  • Khair, Tendu, Axle wood, Palas
  • UP, MP, Rajasthan, Central Deccan
  • More open canopy, grass undergrowth
Why do deciduous trees shed leaves for 6–8 weeks in March–April? To reduce water loss (transpiration) during the dry summer season. The long dry period triggers leaf fall.
The Nilgiri Hills receive 150–180 cm rainfall → moist deciduous forests dominate there. Trees shed leaves during dry season.
🏜️ Tropical Desert / Thorn Forests — Adapted to survive dry conditions ▼

Rainfall: Less than 70 cm/year (some areas < 25 cm). Distribution: Rajasthan, parts of Punjab, Haryana, Gujarat — arid and semi-arid regions.

Key trees: Acacias, Euphorbias, Cacti, Babool, Khejri

Adaptations (Why stunted growth?):

  • Stunted growth — lack of water and nutrients in soil limits growth
  • Thorns instead of leaves — to reduce water loss through transpiration
  • Long tap roots — to reach deep groundwater
  • Thick waxy bark/leaves — to prevent water evaporation
  • Plants are widely spaced due to competition for water
🌊 Littoral / Mangrove Forests — Tidal forests of coastal areas ▼

Location: Along coastal areas, river deltas, estuaries, tidal mudflats. Major region: Sundarbans (West Bengal) — world's largest mangrove forest. Also: Andaman & Nicobar, Mahanadi delta, Krishna delta.

Conditions for growth:

  • Tidal influence — regularly flooded by salty or brackish water
  • Trees can survive in both saline and fresh water
  • Muddy, waterlogged, anaerobic (oxygen-poor) soil

Features:

  • Pneumatophores (breathing roots) — roots that grow above soil surface to get oxygen
  • Dense, interlocked root system (protects coast from erosion and cyclones)
  • Trees: Sundri (in Sundarbans), Rhizophora, Avicennia, Phoenix palms
  • Excellent breeding ground for fish and shrimp
The Sundarbans is famous for the Sundri tree (from which it gets its name) and for being the last stronghold of the Royal Bengal Tiger (Project Tiger).
🏔️ Mountain Vegetation — Changes with altitude ▼

As altitude increases → temperature drops → vegetation changes. This is called altitudinal zonation.

AltitudeTypeTrees/Plants
Below 1,000 mTropical / SubtropicalOak, Chestnuts (in foothills)
1,000 – 2,000 mTemperate (Broadleaf)Oak, Chestnut, Magnolia — in Himalayas and NE India
2,000 – 3,000 mTemperate ConiferousDeodar, Silver fir, Pine, Spruce, Rhododendron

🛡 Forest Conservation
🌱
Social Forestry
Planting trees on common lands, along roads, and river banks by local communities. Main reason for recent increase in forest cover.
🤝
JFM (Joint Forest Management)
Government and local communities jointly manage forests. Communities get benefits in return for protecting forests.
📜
Legal Protection
Wildlife Protection Act 1972; Forest Conservation Act 1980; National Forest Policy 1988 — aims for 33% forest cover
🐯
Project Tiger & Elephant
Special government projects to protect endangered species. 54 Tiger Reserves across India. Elephant corridors protected.
There is a gradual increase in forest cover in India recently — due to promotion of Social Forestry and afforestation drives.

Uncertain Monsoon
Rainfall is seasonal and unreliable — irrigation ensures year-round farming
🌾
Multiple Crops
Irrigation allows farmers to grow 2–3 crops per year instead of 1 rain-fed crop
🏜️
Arid Regions
Parts of Rajasthan, Gujarat get very little rain — only irrigation makes farming possible

Why is South India (like Tamil Nadu) not suitable for canal irrigation? Rivers of S India are non-perennial (seasonal) and the Deccan plateau is rocky — canals can't be dug and rivers don't flow year-round to supply water.
Why are modern methods of irrigation (drip/sprinkler) gaining popularity? 1. Fertilizer/nutrient loss is minimized. 2. Water loss (through evaporation and seepage) is minimum. 3. More efficient use of limited water resources.
☔ Rainwater Harvesting

🏠 Rooftop Rainwater Harvesting

  • Rainwater collected from rooftop of buildings
  • Passed through filters (sand, gravel)
  • Stored in underground tank or used to recharge ground water
  • Common in urban areas — Chennai mandates it

🌿 Surface Runoff Harvesting

  • Rain collected from ground surfaces, roads, fields
  • Directed into ponds, check dams, percolation pits
  • Recharges groundwater table
  • Traditional methods: Johad (Rajasthan), Bamboo drip irrigation (Meghalaya)
🏆 Importance of Rainwater Harvesting
Reduces dependence on piped water Recharges depleting groundwater Reduces flooding in urban areas Low cost, low tech solution Reduces water bills Prevents soil erosion (slows runoff)

🔥 Conventional Energy Resources
🪨 Coal — India's most abundant fossil fuel ▼

Types (best to worst quality):

  • Anthracite: 90%+ carbon, best quality, hard — J&K
  • Bituminous: 60–80% carbon, most widely used, coking coal — Jharkhand, WB
  • Lignite: lowest quality, brown coal — Tamil Nadu (Neyveli)

Distribution:

  • Jharia (Jharkhand) — largest coalfield in India
  • Raniganj (WB) — oldest coalfield
  • Bokaro, Giridih, Karanpura — Jharkhand
  • Singrauli, Satpura — MP

⚠️ Disadvantages

  • Non-renewable, limited reserves
  • Burning releases CO₂, SO₂ → air pollution, acid rain
  • Mining causes land degradation and subsidence
  • Contributes to greenhouse effect and climate change
🛢️ Petroleum — "Liquid Gold" ▼

Why called "Liquid Gold"? Due to its extremely high economic value as an energy source AND as raw material for a huge number of industries (petrochemicals, plastics, pharmaceuticals, fertilizers).

Distribution:

  • Mumbai High — largest offshore oil field (70% of India's production)
  • Digboi (Assam) — oldest oil field in India
  • Gujarat (Ankeleshwar, Kalol, Mehsana)
  • Rajasthan (Barmer — Mangala field, newer discovery)
Disadvantages of petroleum: Non-renewable; burning releases CO₂ and other pollutants; oil spills harm marine life; price is volatile and India imports large amounts.
💨 Natural Gas — Cleaner fossil fuel ▼

Found alongside petroleum fields — Mumbai High, Hazira (Gujarat), Andhra Pradesh (Krishna-Godavari basin), Tripura.

Advantages over coal/petroleum: Burns more cleanly; lower CO₂ emissions; transported via pipelines; used in homes (CNG, LPG) and industries.

HBJ Pipeline — Hazira–Bijaipur–Jagdishpur (longest gas pipeline in India).

  • States: Punjab–Himachal Pradesh border

🌱 Non-Conventional Energy Sources

☀️ Solar Energy

Best states: Rajasthan, Gujarat (highest sunshine hours)
Generation: Photovoltaic (PV) cells convert sunlight to electricity
Advantages: Inexhaustible, no pollution, no fuel cost, ideal for remote areas

💨 Wind Energy

Best states: Tamil Nadu (Muppandal), Gujarat (Jaisalmer), Rajasthan, Maharashtra
Generation: Wind turbines spin to generate electricity
Advantages: Clean, renewable, can be combined with farming

🌊 Tidal Energy

Best locations: Gulf of Khambhat, Gulf of Kutch (Gujarat), Sundarbans (WB)
Generation: Tidal water movement spins turbines
Advantages: Predictable, no fuel needed, no emissions

🌋 Geothermal Energy

Best locations: Parvati Valley, Tattapani (Himachal Pradesh), Puga valley (J&K)
Generation: Steam from earth's interior drives turbines
Advantages: Constant supply, clean, no fuel cost

⚛️ Nuclear Energy

Fuel: Uranium, Thorium
Plants: Tarapur (Maharashtra — 1st), Rana Pratap Sagar (Rajasthan), Kalpakkam (TN), Narora (UP)
Advantages: High power output, no air pollution during operation

🐄 Biogas

Source: Decomposition of organic waste (cattle dung, crop waste, kitchen waste)
Generation: Methane gas produced by anaerobic bacteria → burns to generate energy
Advantages: Uses waste, no pollution, cheap, slurry used as fertilizer

Why is India ideal for solar power? India is a tropical country — most parts have 300+ sunny days per year. Especially Rajasthan has maximum sunshine hours.

🌍 Importance of Agriculture

⚠️ Problems & Reforms

Problems

  • Small and fragmented land holdings
  • Dependence on uncertain monsoon
  • Lack of irrigation in most areas
  • Low use of technology and HYV seeds
  • Inadequate credit and market access
  • Soil degradation and erosion

Reforms / Solutions

  • Green Revolution — HYV seeds, fertilizers, irrigation
  • NABARD — agricultural credit and finance
  • Land reforms — consolidation of holdings
  • PM Fasal Bima Yojana — crop insurance
  • Multipurpose projects for irrigation
  • Agricultural universities and research

💥 Impact of Waste Accumulation
👁️
Landscape Spoilage
Waste dumps and littering make areas ugly; open landfills create unsightly mounds; debris affects tourism
💨
Air Pollution
Open burning of waste releases CO, CO₂, dioxins, toxic fumes; methane from landfills is a greenhouse gas
🤒
Health Hazards
Garbage breeding ground for mosquitoes, flies, rats → malaria, dengue, cholera, typhoid; leachate contaminates groundwater
🌿
Terrestrial Life
Plastic and chemicals in soil kill micro-organisms; reduces soil fertility; animals eat plastic and die
🐟
Fresh Water Life
Industrial waste kills fish; excess nutrients cause eutrophication (algae bloom, oxygen depletion); rivers and lakes become toxic
🐋
Marine Life
Plastic in oceans accumulates — Great Pacific Garbage Patch; marine animals mistake plastic for food; oil spills harm sea birds and fish
🗑️ Methods of Waste Disposal
🔬 Segregation — First Step ▼

Separating waste at source into categories before disposal — makes all other methods more effective.

🟢 Biodegradable (Wet)

  • Kitchen waste (vegetable/fruit peels)
  • Food scraps, garden waste
  • Paper, natural cloth
  • → Can be composted

🔴 Non-Biodegradable (Dry)

  • Plastics, synthetic fibres
  • Glass, metals, batteries
  • E-waste (old phones, circuits)
  • → Must be recycled or safely buried
🏗️ Dumping — Landfills ▼

Open Dump: Waste simply left in open areas — dangerous (attracts pests, pollutes groundwater, fires). This is the least safe method.

Sanitary Landfill: More scientific method — waste compacted and covered with layers of soil; liners prevent leachate from entering groundwater; methane collected for energy. Better but still not ideal.

Landfills take up large areas of land, cannot be built on for years, and still pollute surroundings. Composting and recycling are always preferred.
🌱 Composting — Turning Waste into Resource ▼

Organic (biodegradable) waste is decomposed by micro-organisms under controlled conditions to produce compost — a nutrient-rich natural fertilizer.

Methods: Pit composting, vermicomposting (using earthworms), biogas plants

Benefits: Returns nutrients to soil; reduces dependence on chemical fertilizers; reduces waste volume significantly; cheap and simple

🔥 Incineration — Burning at High Temperature ▼

Waste burned at very high temperatures (800–1200°C) in special incinerators. Reduces waste volume by 90%. Used mainly for hospital/medical waste and hazardous waste.

Advantage: Reduces volume drastically; can generate energy (waste-to-energy plants)

Disadvantage: Releases toxic fumes (dioxins) if not properly filtered; expensive

🔄 The 3Rs — Best Approach to Waste ⭐ Always Asked
🚫
REDUCE
Use less in the first place.

Examples: Avoid single-use plastics; buy only what you need; choose products with less packaging; turn off lights/water when not in use.
🔁
REUSE
Use items again before throwing away.

Examples: Refill water bottles; use cloth bags; repair broken items instead of replacing; donate old clothes and books.
♻️
RECYCLE
Convert waste into new products.

Examples: Paper → recycled paper; Glass → new glass; Aluminium → new cans; Plastic → furniture/pipes. Creates jobs and reduces mining.
Order of priority: Reduce > Reuse > Recycle. Reducing waste at the source is always better than dealing with it later. Recycling still uses energy — it's the last resort of the 3Rs.

🌤 Climate — Top Points

Click to expand ▼
  • India has Tropical Monsoon Climate — seasonal reversal of winds is its key feature
  • Monsoon = periodic wind; SW in summer (rain-bearing), NE in winter (dry)
  • Low pressure over NW India in summer → pulls in ocean winds → SW Monsoon begins
  • Mawsynram (Meghalaya) = highest rainfall in world (~1187 cm/yr) due to funnel-shaped Khasi Hills
  • Tamil Nadu gets rain during Retreating Monsoon (Oct–Nov) via NE winds from Bay of Bengal
  • Coastal cities (Mumbai) have moderate temp range; interior cities (Delhi) have extreme range
  • Goa > Puducherry in summer rainfall: Goa = windward side of Western Ghats
  • Loo = hot dry wind in N India during summer (May–June)
  • Kalbaisakhi (Nor'westers) = pre-monsoon showers in WB, Assam — helps tea, jute, rice
  • Summer season: March–May | Monsoon: June–September | Retreating: Oct–Nov | Winter: Dec–Feb

🌍 Soil — Top Points

Click to expand ▼
  • Alluvial = most fertile, most widespread; rich in potash; deficient in nitrogen
  • Khadar = new alluvial (near rivers, more fertile); Bhangar = old alluvial (kankar nodules)
  • Black soil formed from weathering of basic igneous rocks (basalt/Deccan Trap)
  • Black soil = self-ploughing; retains moisture; ideal for cotton
  • Red soil = red because of iron oxide; poor in nitrogen and humus; dry farming preferred
  • Laterite = formed by leaching in heavy rain; hard when dry → used as building material
  • Laterite crops: Tea, Coffee, Cashew (with manure addition)
  • Terrace farming = ideal for hilly regions (slows runoff, holds soil)
  • Wind erosion common in arid regions — low moisture, sparse vegetation, wind lifts topsoil
  • Deepening river beds prevents flooding and therefore shore/bank erosion

🌿 Natural Vegetation — Top Points

Click to expand ▼
  • Tropical Evergreen: >200 cm rain; Ebony, Mahogany; no definite leafless period
  • Evergreen forests are hard to exploit: dense, no transport, great variety, heavy hardwood
  • Deciduous forests = most widespread in India (~60%); shed leaves for 6–8 weeks in March–April
  • Teak = most commercially valuable deciduous tree
  • Desert/Thorn forests: <70 cm rain; stunted growth due to lack of water and minerals
  • Littoral/Mangrove: tidal areas; Sundarbans = world's largest mangrove; Sundri tree
  • Mangrove roots = pneumatophores (above-ground roots for oxygen)
  • Mountain vegetation changes with altitude — altitudinal zonation
  • Increase in forest cover recently due to Social Forestry programs
  • JFM = Joint Forest Management (communities protect forests in exchange for benefits)

💧 Water Resources — Top Points

Click to expand ▼
  • Modern irrigation methods (drip, sprinkler) minimize water loss and fertilizer loss
  • Canal irrigation: flat plains, perennial rivers (Punjab, UP, Haryana)
  • Tank irrigation: rocky Deccan Plateau (TN, Karnataka, AP) — rivers are non-perennial
  • Tube wells popular in Punjab: alluvial soil → easy drilling; high water table; reliable
  • South India not suited for canal irrigation: rocky, non-perennial rivers
  • Rooftop RWH = rain collected from rooftop → filtered → stored → recharges groundwater
  • RWH importance: reduces piped water dependence; recharges depleting aquifers; cheap
  • Inundation canal = canal from river without a dam/barrage to control flow
  • Irrigation essential: monsoon is uncertain, unreliable; needed for Rabi crops in dry winter

⛏️ Minerals & Energy — Top Points

Click to expand ▼
  • Black soil formed from igneous rocks; Copper used for electric wires → good conductor
  • Manganese from Balaghat, MP; Copper from Khetri, Rajasthan
  • Iron ore: Magnetite (best, 72%); Hematite (most commercial)
  • Jharia (Jharkhand) = largest coalfield; Digboi (Assam) = oldest oil field; Mumbai High = largest offshore

  • Biogas: organic waste → methane by anaerobic bacteria; slurry = fertilizer

🌾 Agriculture — Top Points

Click to expand ▼
  • Kharif = June–Sep (rice, cotton, jute, sugarcane); Rabi = Oct–Mar (wheat, mustard, gram)
  • Wheat confined to N India → temperate crop, needs cool (10–15°C) winters
  • Cotton needs black regur soil and dry weather during boll formation
  • Gujarat = largest cotton producer; UP = largest sugarcane producer
  • Jute = "Golden Fibre"; WB = 80% of production; processing = retting
  • Tea: hill slopes, Assam (largest); well-drained acidic soil; processing → pluck→wither→roll→ferment→dry
  • Coffee: Karnataka = 70%; Arabica (better quality), Robusta; needs shade
  • Sugar factories near fields: sucrose drops within 24 hours of harvesting
  • Ratoon cropping: old sugarcane roots regrow → matures earlier, costs less ⭐
  • Mixed farming gives security: if crops fail, livestock income compensates
  • Shifting cultivation (Jhum) — NE India; causes soil erosion; traditional but destructive

🚆 Transport — Top Points

Click to expand ▼
  • Roadways important: low cost, door-to-door, can reach all terrain including high altitudes
  • 70% of Indians don't use airways → too expensive
  • S Indian rivers not for inland waterways: non-perennial, swift-flowing over plateau
  • Water transport not popular: slow, limited routes, seasonal

♻️ Waste Management — Top Points

Click to expand ▼
  • Waste accumulation causes: landscape spoilage, air/water pollution, health hazards, harm to all ecosystems
  • Eutrophication = excess nutrients → algae bloom → oxygen depletion → aquatic life dies (fresh water)
  • Segregation = separating biodegradable (wet) and non-biodegradable (dry) waste at source
  • Composting: organic waste decomposed by microbes → nutrient-rich compost/manure
  • Sanitary landfill > open dump; waste compacted, covered with soil, leachate lined
  • 3Rs priority order: Reduce > Reuse > Recycle
  • Reduce = use less; Reuse = use again; Recycle = convert to new product
  • Recycling still uses energy — reducing at source is always best
  • E-waste (electronics) is hazardous — contains lead, mercury; needs special disposal
  • Plastics in oceans accumulate in marine food chains (bioaccumulation) harming all marine life
